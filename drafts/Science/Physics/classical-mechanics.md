# Classical Mechanics

Classical mechanics is the branch of physics that deals with the motion of macroscopic objects under the influence of forces. Developed primarily by Isaac Newton in the 17th century, it remained the dominant framework for understanding the physical world until the advent of quantum mechanics and relativity in the 20th century.

## Newton's Laws of Motion

1. **First Law (Inertia)**: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.
2. **Second Law**: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass: **F = ma**.
3. **Third Law**: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

## Kinematics

Kinematics describes motion without considering its causes. Key equations for constant acceleration include:

- v = v0 + at
- x = x0 + v0*t + (1/2)*a*t^2
- v^2 = v0^2 + 2*a*(x - x0)

where v is velocity, a is acceleration, t is time, and x is position.

## Energy and Work

- **Kinetic Energy**: K = (1/2)mv^2
- **Potential Energy**: U = mgh (gravitational, near Earth's surface)
- **Work-Energy Theorem**: The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy
- **Conservation of Energy**: In an isolated system, total mechanical energy remains constant

## Momentum

- **Linear Momentum**: p = mv
- **Conservation of Momentum**: In the absence of external forces, total momentum is conserved
- **Impulse**: J = F * delta_t = delta_p

## Lagrangian Mechanics

An alternative formulation using the Lagrangian L = T - V (kinetic minus potential energy). The equations of motion are derived from the **Euler-Lagrange equation**. This approach is particularly powerful for systems with constraints and generalizes naturally to other areas of physics.

## Limitations

Classical mechanics breaks down at very high speeds (where special relativity applies), at very small scales (where quantum mechanics dominates), and in strong gravitational fields (where general relativity is needed).

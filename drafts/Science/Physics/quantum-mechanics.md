# Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is the fundamental theory in physics that describes the behavior of nature at and below the scale of atoms. It is the foundation of all quantum physics, including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

## Historical Development

Quantum mechanics emerged in the early 20th century from the inability of classical physics to explain certain phenomena. Key milestones include Max Planck's quantization of energy (1900), Albert Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect (1905), Niels Bohr's model of the atom (1913), and the development of matrix mechanics by Werner Heisenberg and wave mechanics by Erwin Schrodinger in the 1920s.

## Core Principles

### Wave-Particle Duality

All matter exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties. Electrons can produce interference patterns like waves, yet they also interact as discrete particles. This duality is described by the **de Broglie wavelength**.

### The Uncertainty Principle

Formulated by Heisenberg, this principle states that certain pairs of physical properties (like position and momentum) cannot both be precisely determined simultaneously. This is not a limitation of measurement but a fundamental property of nature.

### Quantum Superposition

A quantum system can exist in multiple states simultaneously until it is measured. The famous thought experiment of **Schrodinger's cat** illustrates this concept: the cat is simultaneously alive and dead until observed.

### Quantum Entanglement

When two particles become entangled, the quantum state of one instantly influences the other, regardless of the distance between them. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance." Entanglement is a key resource in quantum computing and quantum cryptography.

## The Schrodinger Equation

The central equation of quantum mechanics describes how the quantum state of a system evolves over time. The time-independent form for a particle in a potential V(x) is:

**H|psi> = E|psi>**

where H is the Hamiltonian operator, |psi> is the wave function, and E is the energy eigenvalue.

## Applications

Quantum mechanics underlies virtually all modern technology: semiconductors and transistors, lasers, MRI machines, atomic clocks, and the emerging fields of quantum computing and quantum cryptography.
